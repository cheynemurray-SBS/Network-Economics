Value in Scarcity vs. Value in Plentitude

How a free photo-sharing app became worth a billion dollars in under two years — without ever making anything rare.

Warm-Up The Instagram Question

In 2012, Facebook acquired Instagram — a small mobile app with no factories, no inventory, and no physical product — for roughly one billion dollars. The app was barely two years old and had never turned a profit.

Think about it before you read on

How does a company with nothing to manufacture, mine, or ship generate that kind of value? What exactly were investors paying for?

The answer requires unlearning the logic that governs most of the physical economy: the logic of scarcity.

Core Concept Two Logics of Value

Traditional economics teaches that value rises as supply falls. Digital network economics often works the opposite way — value rises as the number of participants grows. Both logics are real; they just apply to different kinds of assets.

Physical / Traditional Assets

Value in Scarcity

Worth increases as availability decreases.

  • A diamond's value comes from its rarity and unique physical beauty.
  • The fewer that exist, the higher the price buyers will pay.
  • Value is tied to the object itself, independent of how many other people own one.

Digital / Network Assets

Value in Plentitude

Worth increases as adoption increases.

  • Instagram's "first copy" would be worth nothing to a buyer on its own.
  • Its value comes from the size of its active network and what members share within it.
  • Value is tied to connections between people — relationships, reputation, content, and communication.

The takeaway for managers: before you evaluate a digital product or platform, ask whether its value model is scarcity-based or network-based. Applying scarcity thinking to a network asset — or vice versa — leads to badly mispriced strategy decisions.

Core Concept Physical vs. Virtual Networks

Both types of networks derive value from connections between nodes — but the nature of that connection differs.

Physical Networks

Nodes are connected by actual, visible infrastructure — think highways, power grids, telephone lines, or ATM networks. The connection has a physical, tangible form.

Virtual Networks

Nodes are connected invisibly — through software, protocols, and platforms. Instagram, LinkedIn, and Venmo are virtual networks: no wires connect the users, but the network still enables sharing and communication.

On virtual networks specifically, what gets shared isn't limited to money or physical goods — it includes multimedia, expertise, opinions, reputation, and social capital. This is why a "free" app can still generate enormous value: the currency being exchanged is attention, data, and relationships, not cash.

Not yet saved

Facebook wasn't buying a product in the traditional sense — there was no factory, patent, or physical asset that made Instagram scarce or hard to replicate. What it was buying was a fully-formed network: tens of millions of active users, the connections between them, and the habits and attention already built around the app.

That network came with three things money can't easily buy from scratch: an existing social graph (who follows whom), a stream of user-generated content that keeps people coming back, and a distribution channel Facebook could eventually monetize through advertising. In other words, Facebook paid for plentitude itself — the size and density of the network — not for any single copy of the software.

There's no single right answer here since your example is personal — but here's how I'd reason through one: take a platform like Zoom or LinkedIn. Neither has any real scarcity to sell; anyone can create an account for free. Their value comes entirely from plentitude — the more colleagues, clients, or contacts already on the platform, the more useful it becomes for you to join too. That's a network effect.

Compare that to something like a limited-edition sneaker drop sold through an app. The app itself is a distribution tool, but the underlying product still derives its value from scarcity — only a fixed number exist. The lesson: don't judge the value logic by the technology (app vs. no app); judge it by whether more participants make the offering more valuable (plentitude) or whether fewer available units make it more valuable (scarcity). Some products even blend both.

No — bigger is not automatically better, and this is a common misreading of network effects. Network effects describe how the value of a network to its members grows with size. They say nothing about whether the cost of running that network grows at the same, slower, or faster rate.

In practice, very large networks run into diminishing returns: marketing no longer gets cheaper per new user once you've saturated the easy-to-reach audience, and internal costs — coordination across teams, content moderation, customer support, management layers — start rising just to keep the larger network functioning. That's why the smartest strategic question isn't "how do we get as big as possible," but "where is the point at which added scale stops adding more value than it costs to manage?" This is also why not every market tips toward one dominant winner — firms serving a well-defined niche well can out-earn firms chasing raw scale.

Core Concept Tipping Points & Tippy Markets

Networks with strong positive feedback — where more users make the product more valuable, which attracts even more users — behave very differently from ordinary markets. Two terms describe this dynamic:

Tippy Market

A market subject to strong positive feedback loops, where growth accelerates rather than levels off as adoption increases.

Tipping Point

The moment a market reaches critical mass and one player begins to dominate — sometimes called the "watershed of dominance," because from that point forward it's largely winner-take-all.

Not every market tips

New members don't automatically add value for everyone. Compare Garmin GPS devices (not tippy — customers have varied, niche needs a single winner can't satisfy) with something like a ride-sharing network (tippy — more riders and more drivers make the service better for everyone on it).

The variety of customer needs is what preserves distinct market niches even inside categories that otherwise show network effects. Managers should ask: does adding one more customer make the product genuinely better for every existing customer, or does it simply add another user without shared benefit? The answer determines whether a market is tippy — and whether "winning big fast" or "serving a niche well" is the smarter strategy.
